## Step 1: Voice Dictation

The first thing that will surprise people: **I rarely type my prompts. I dictate them.**

I use Wispr Flow for voice-to-text dictation. It is not an interactive voice mode — I am not having a conversation with the AI through a microphone. I am dictating text into the prompt field, the same way you might dictate a message or an email.

I was deeply skeptical of this at first. I am a fast typist. I have always been comfortable expressing technical ideas through text. But dictation changed the quality of my prompts in a way I did not expect.

Here is why: **typing imposes a cognitive overhead that you do not notice until it is gone.** When you type, part of your brain is occupied with the mechanical act of translating thoughts into keystrokes. You unconsciously edit as you go. You shorten your prompts because typing is effort. You structure them prematurely because the linear nature of typing forces you to commit to an order.

When you dictate, all of that disappears. You just *talk*. You ramble. You describe the problem from three different angles. You mention the thing you are worried about, then circle back to the context, then add the constraint you almost forgot. It is messy and unstructured and that is exactly the point.

The AI does not care about structure. It is excellent at taking a five-paragraph stream of consciousness and extracting the intent. Your job is to convey as much context and nuance as possible. Dictation lets you do that with dramatically less effort.

My typical prompt is four to five paragraphs long. It describes what I am trying to build, points to specific files in the codebase, explains the current state of things, mentions constraints and edge cases I am thinking about, and states what I want the agent to do. Writing that by hand would take five to ten minutes and feel like work. **Dictating it takes sixty seconds and feels like thinking out loud.**

<Callout type="tip">
If you try nothing else from this post, try dictating your prompts for a week. It will change your relationship with AI tools.
</Callout>

## Step 2: The Two-Phase Prompt

This is the **single most important technique** in my workflow, and almost nobody does it.

When I want an AI agent to make changes to my codebase, I never start by asking for changes. I start by asking for *understanding*.

**Phase 1: Explore and explain.** The first prompt is always something like:

> Go look at the authentication module in `src/auth/`. Read through the files, understand how the flow works, and describe back to me what you see. Feel free to ask me clarifying questions if anything is unclear.

That is it. No instructions to change anything. No feature request. Just: look at this and tell me what you understand.

The agent will read the files, trace the logic, and come back with a description of how the system works. Sometimes it is spot-on. Sometimes it misses a nuance. Sometimes it asks questions that reveal it does not understand a key constraint. **This is exactly the information you need before asking it to make changes.**

**Phase 2: Execute with context.** Only after I am confident the agent understands the current state do I give it the actual task:

> Great, that is correct. Now here is what I need: add support for OAuth providers. The user should be able to link multiple OAuth accounts to a single profile. Here are the scenarios that need to work...

By this point, the agent has a mental model of the existing code. It knows where things live, how they connect, what patterns are in use. The implementation it produces is **dramatically better** than what you get from a cold start.

There are a few key phrases I use constantly in Phase 1:

- **"Be thorough."** This prevents the agent from skimming and giving you a shallow summary.
- **"Be critical."** This activates a self-review mode where the agent actually evaluates what it sees rather than just describing it.
- **"Feel free to ask me clarifying questions."** This opens a dialogue. The agent will ask things like "I see two different patterns for handling errors — which one should I follow?" and those questions are *gold*.

<Philosophy>
The two-phase approach works because it mirrors how a good human collaborator operates. You would never hand a colleague a task without first making sure they understand the context. Extend the same courtesy to your AI agent.
</Philosophy>

## Step 3: Interface-First Design

Before I ask an AI to build anything complex, I write a short specification document. Not code. A Markdown file that describes:

- The directory structure for the new feature
- The key interfaces and type signatures
- Pseudo-code (not real code) for the core behavior
- The patterns the implementation should follow

Here is a simplified example of what this looks like:

```markdown
## Feature: Notification System

### Directory Structure
src/notifications/
  types.ts          — Notification types and interfaces
  store.ts          — Notification state management
  dispatcher.ts     — Core dispatch logic
  channels/
    in-app.ts       — In-app notification channel
    email.ts        — Email notification channel
    push.ts         — Push notification channel

### Core Interface
NotificationDispatcher receives a NotificationEvent, determines
which channels should fire based on user preferences, and dispatches
to each channel in parallel. Failed channels should retry up to 3 times
with exponential backoff. All dispatches are logged.

### Function Signatures
dispatchNotification(event: NotificationEvent): Promise<DispatchResult>
resolveChannels(event: NotificationEvent, prefs: UserPrefs): Channel[]
executeChannel(channel: Channel, event: NotificationEvent): Promise<void>

### Patterns
- Follow the same error handling pattern as src/sync/operations.ts
- Use the existing logger from src/lib/logger.ts
- Channel implementations should follow the adapter pattern
  used in src/integrations/
```

Why does this work so well? Because **AI excels at pattern matching**. When you give it a clear interface, a defined directory structure, and explicit patterns to follow, you have constrained the solution space to the point where the implementation almost writes itself. The agent matches the patterns you pointed to, fills in the implementation behind your interfaces, and produces code that fits naturally into the existing codebase.

Your job is to be the architect. Define the shape of the system. Let the AI fill in the details.

## Step 4: Scenario-Driven Development

This is where most people go wrong with AI development. They ask for a feature, get the code, and then try to figure out if it works. **Flip it around.** Define what "works" means *before* the code is written.

For every feature, I define an explicit list of scenarios:

```
The scenarios for this feature are:
1. User creates a new notification preference
2. User receives an in-app notification when a relevant event fires
3. User with email enabled receives both in-app and email notifications
4. User with a disabled channel does not receive notifications on that channel
5. Failed notification delivery retries up to 3 times
6. After 3 failures, the notification is marked as failed and logged
7. Multiple notifications dispatched in parallel do not interfere with each other
```

Then I ask the agent to do something specific: enumerate all the scenarios it can think of (it often catches ones I missed), and then **dry-run each scenario through the code mentally**.

> Walk through each scenario step by step through the code. For each one, trace the execution path from entry point to completion. Tell me if any scenario would fail, and why.

This dry-run step is remarkably effective. The agent will trace the logic, find that scenario 5 does not work because the retry counter is not being decremented correctly, and fix it — all before I have even opened a browser.

Only after the dry run looks clean do we move to actual testing.

<Callout type="note">
The dry-run step is where the agent catches most bugs. By tracing each scenario through the code *before* running it, you avoid the slow debug-fix-rerun cycle entirely.
</Callout>

## Step 5: Browser Testing with MCP

This is the step that completes the feedback loop, and it is the reason my workflow produces reliable results.

I use two Model Context Protocol (MCP) servers: **Playwright MCP** and **Chrome DevTools MCP**. Together, they give the AI agent the ability to interact with a running application in a browser — clicking elements, filling forms, navigating pages, and reading the DOM.

Here is how I use it:

> The app is running on localhost:3000. Go through each of the notification scenarios we defined. For each scenario, perform the actions in the browser, check the console for any errors, and verify the expected behavior. Add console logs if you need them for validation. Report back on which scenarios pass and which fail.

The agent opens the browser, performs the actions, reads the console output, and reports back. If something fails, it debugs the issue — often by adding console logs, checking the state, tracing the problem — exactly the way a human developer would debug.

This creates a **self-correction loop**. The agent finds a bug, fixes the code, re-runs the scenario, and continues until all scenarios pass. I am usually doing something else during this time.

<Callout type="important">
The number one reason people get bad results from AI coding is an **incomplete feedback loop**. When the agent cannot see, test, or validate its own output, it is working blind. Give it the same tools a human developer uses — a browser, a console, the ability to inspect and interact — and the quality of output improves dramatically.
</Callout>

## Step 6: Review Like a PR

After the agent finishes and all scenarios pass, I do not immediately merge the changes. **I walk away.**

I come back twenty to thirty minutes later with fresh eyes and review the git diff. I treat it exactly like I would treat a pull request from a colleague. I read through the changes, and I ask questions:

- "Have we handled the case where the user has no notification preferences set?"
- "Does this change affect the existing email integration tests?"
- "What happens if the notification service is down when we try to dispatch?"

The agent answers these questions, sometimes revealing gaps, sometimes confirming that the edge case is already handled. If there are gaps, we fix them. If not, we are done.

I call this the *"let it cook"* philosophy. Give a detailed prompt, walk away, come back and review. Just like you would not stand behind a colleague watching them type, do not sit and watch the AI generate code line by line. Trust the process, then verify the output.

## Step 7: Cross-Model Review

This is one of the more unusual parts of my workflow, and **it catches real bugs**.

After Claude Code finishes a feature, I will sometimes ask a different model — OpenAI's Codex, for instance — to review the work. The prompt is deliberately framed to provoke thoroughness:

> Claude wrote the following code to implement a notification dispatch system. Review it critically and point out any bugs, edge cases, or design issues.

There is an interesting dynamic here. The reviewing model tends to be *thorough and competitive*, finding real issues that the original model missed. Maybe it is the framing, maybe it is the different training data, but cross-model review consistently surfaces things that single-model development does not.

Then I feed the review back to the original model:

> Codex found the following issues in your notification dispatch implementation. Address each one.

The original model takes the feedback seriously and addresses each point. I run this loop — implement, review with a different model, feed back, fix — until the issues converge to zero. Usually that takes **two to three rounds**.

<Callout type="tip">
Framing the review as "Model X wrote this, find the problems" triggers a more thorough review than asking the same model to self-review. Use the competitive dynamic to your advantage.
</Callout>

## Step 8: Parallel Workflows

Once you are comfortable with the workflow above, the natural next step is **running multiple agents in parallel**.

My setup: I clone the repository into three separate directories. I run a separate Claude Code session in each. I work on three different tasks simultaneously.

The rhythm works like this: I give Agent 1 a detailed prompt for Feature A. While it works, I give Agent 2 a prompt for Feature B. When Agent 1 comes back with questions, I switch to it and answer while Agent 3 starts on Feature C. It is like managing three junior developers who are all fast, knowledgeable, but need direction.

**Three agents is the sweet spot.** Fewer and you have idle time. More and the context-switching overhead starts to hurt. With three, there is almost always one agent ready for your input while the other two are working.

## The Codebase Hygiene Checklist

None of the above works without a well-maintained codebase. Here is what I consider non-negotiable for AI-assisted development:

**Linting.** I use Biome. It catches style and correctness issues automatically. More importantly, it gives the AI agent a fast signal about whether its code meets the project standards. The agent can run the linter, see the errors, and self-correct.

**TypeScript in strict mode.** Type safety gives both you and the AI confidence in correctness. The compiler catches a huge class of errors before anything runs. When the agent writes code that does not type-check, it knows immediately.

**Tests.** The safety net that lets you move fast. When the agent can run the test suite and see what passes and what fails, tests become part of the feedback loop.

**Git.** Always. Diffs are your review tool. The ability to see exactly what changed, revert if something goes wrong, and track the evolution of the codebase is essential. I review git diffs after every AI-generated change.

These tools complete the feedback loop. The AI can run the linter, check types, run tests, and self-correct based on the results. Without them, you are relying on the AI to be perfect on the first try. It will not be.

## A Concrete Example: The Bundle Size Analyzer

Let me walk through a real example of this workflow end to end. I needed a GitHub Action that compares bundle sizes between the main branch and a pull request, leaves a formatted comment on the PR, and adds status checks. This is the kind of infrastructure work that would have taken me days of reading GitHub Actions documentation, experimenting with YAML syntax, and debugging CI failures.

**I built it in about an hour.**

**The spec:** I wrote a short description of what I wanted. A workflow that triggers on pull requests and pushes to main. On pushes to main, it saves a baseline. On pull requests, it compares against the baseline and posts a comment.

**The two-phase prompt:** I started by having the agent explore the existing CI setup and the project's build tooling. It understood we were using Rsbuild, that the monorepo had an `apps/app` directory, and that Bun was the package manager.

**The implementation:** The agent produced a complete workflow file. Here is a fragment that shows the structure:

```yaml
on:
  pull_request:
    types: [opened, synchronize, reopened]
    paths:
      - "apps/app/**"
      - "packages/**"
      - "bun.lock"
      - "turbo.json"
      - "package.json"
  push:
    branches:
      - main
```

It triggers only on relevant file changes — no wasted CI minutes. On push to main, it builds, generates a baseline, and caches it:

```yaml
- name: Generate baseline
  if: github.event_name == 'push'
  run: bun scripts/analyze-bundle.js --output ../../bundle-baseline.json

- name: Save baseline to cache
  if: github.event_name == 'push'
  uses: actions/cache/save@v5
  with:
    path: bundle-baseline.json
    key: bundle-baseline-main-${{ github.sha }}
```

On pull requests, it restores the baseline from cache and runs a comparison:

```yaml
- name: Restore baseline from cache
  if: github.event_name == 'pull_request'
  uses: actions/cache/restore@v5
  with:
    path: bundle-baseline.json
    key: bundle-baseline-main-
    restore-keys: |
      bundle-baseline-main-

- name: Run bundle analysis
  if: github.event_name == 'pull_request'
  run: |
    BASELINE_ARG=""
    if [ -f ../../bundle-baseline.json ]; then
      BASELINE_ARG="--baseline ../../bundle-baseline.json"
    fi
    bun scripts/analyze-bundle.js $BASELINE_ARG \
      --output ../../bundle-report.json \
      --markdown ../../bundle-comment.md
```

But here is where it gets interesting. I then had the agent add a second job — an **AI-powered bundle review** — that downloads the analysis data and uses Claude to review the PR for bundle size regressions:

```yaml
bundle-review:
  if: github.event_name == 'pull_request'
  needs: bundle-analysis
  runs-on: ubuntu-latest
  steps:
    - name: Run Bundle Doctor Review
      uses: anthropics/claude-code-action@v1
      with:
        prompt: |
          You are Bundle Doctor, a frontend bundle size reviewer.
          Analyze this PR for bundle size impact...
```

An AI agent running inside a CI pipeline, reviewing bundle changes, leaving PR comments with optimization suggestions. The entire workflow — the YAML, the analysis script, the Rsdoctor integration, the source map explorer report, the artifact uploads, the cache strategy, the AI review step — was built in a single session.

The point is not that AI wrote some YAML. The point is that **the entire system** — multiple jobs, conditional logic, artifact management, caching strategy, AI integration — came together in an hour because the workflow I described above (spec, two-phase prompt, scenario testing, review) compresses the feedback loop so dramatically.

## The Honest Assessment

I want to end with honesty, because the hype around AI coding is thick and not all of it is warranted.

**AI still produces garbage sometimes.** This has not gone away. Single-shot features are a myth — you will always need to steer, iterate, and correct. Think of the AI as an intern who has read every programming book ever written but has zero context about your specific project. It needs hand-holding, guidance, and direction.

The key is not to expect perfection. It is to build a system — feedback loops, testing, review — that catches and corrects mistakes efficiently. The workflow I have described is not about making AI perfect. It is about **creating an environment where AI can iterate to correctness quickly**.

And the hand-holding pays off. Once you give the agent the right context — once you walk through Phase 1, once you point it at the right files, once you define the scenarios clearly — it *blooms*. It delivers exactly what you want, often surprising you with approaches you would not have considered.

<Philosophy>
The developers who get the most out of AI are not the ones using the fanciest tools. They are the ones who have learned how to communicate clearly with their agent, how to define what "done" looks like, and how to build the infrastructure that lets the agent verify its own work.
</Philosophy>

That is the whole workflow. Voice dictation for input. Two-phase prompts for understanding. Interface-first design for architecture. Scenario-driven development for correctness. Browser testing for validation. PR-style review for quality. Cross-model review for thoroughness. Parallel workflows for throughput. And a well-maintained codebase to make all of it possible.

Start with one step. Try dictating your next prompt. Try the two-phase approach on your next feature. You do not need to adopt everything at once. But once you feel the difference, you will not go back.
