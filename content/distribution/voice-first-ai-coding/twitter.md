## Post 1

I thought voice dictation for coding was a gimmick.

I like keyboards.
I type quickly.
Talking to my computer felt vague, clumsy, and slightly embarrassing.

Then AI coding showed me what I had missed:

I did not have a typing problem.
I had a context-budget problem.

---

## Post 2

Every time I typed a prompt,
I unconsciously optimized for brevity.

I would skip the file path.
Avoid explaining the tradeoff.
Trust the model to infer the edge case.

Not because I wanted to be vague.
Because typing five paragraphs of precise context feels expensive.

---

## Post 3

That is why typed prompts often go wrong.

You know:

- where the relevant code already lives
- what pattern the new code should follow
- what edge case is probably going to break
- what you definitely do not want the model to change

---

## Post 4

Then you type:

> Add a dark mode toggle to the header

That is not a thinking failure.
It is a transmission failure.

---

## Post 5

If you were briefing a new developer on your team,
you would never stop there.

You would mention the file,
the existing hook,
the styles to match,
the constraint not to break mobile,
the pattern the change should follow.

That is not better prompt engineering.
It is just fuller context.

---

## Post 6

Voice changes that because saying the extra sentence feels cheap.
And then the extra sentence after that.

That is the real win.

Not because speaking is faster.
Because it removes friction between what you know and what the model needs to know.

---

## Post 7

Typing trained a lot of us to be terse.
AI rewards being clear.
